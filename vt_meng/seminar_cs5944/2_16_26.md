This seminar explores how traditional robot design and control pipelines are often expensive, rigid, and inefficient, which motivates a co-design approach that optimizes both physical structure and control policies. Instead of separating modeling and control, the speaker demonstrates that end-to-end learned systems produce better performance and more favorable optimization landscapes than classical model-based methods. This is particularly evident in modular multi-robot systems designed for terrain traversal, where robots dynamically couple and decouple depending on the environment. A key insight is that rigid designs fail to generalize across terrain types, while adaptive coupling mechanisms and distributed control—where each robot treats others as part of the environment—scale more effectively. The speaker then highlights how co-design enables nonintuitive but highly effective solutions. In a soft gripper case study, jointly optimizing tendon routing and stiffness using neural physics models leads to asymmetric designs that outperform traditional soft or rigid alternatives. These learned models provide smoother gradients than classical contact-based simulations, improving convergence during optimization. Scaling up both the design space and number of agents further enhances performance. Additionally, incorporating humans into the loop—such as using teleoperation to guide geometric design—allows systems to align with human intuition while still discovering novel capabilities, including robotic joints that exceed human motion constraints. The pipeline extends to generating robot hands from human motion data by mapping trajectories into latent representations, which are then decoded into optimized mechanical designs. Future directions include learning structured latent spaces for design, integrating hardware-in-the-loop optimization, and automating fabrication processes such as 3D printing paths.

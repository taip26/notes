## Summary start

This seminar combines a teaching demonstration on hashing with research on student help-seeking behavior in computing education. The teaching portion introduces hash tables as key-value data structures indexed by a hash function, highlighting both their efficiency and limitations. The speaker walks through examples showing how collisions arise and how poor key distributions or large key ranges can lead to inefficiencies such as wasted space compared to alternatives like BSTs or linked lists. Various hashing strategies are discussed, including modulo-based methods, binning, and mid-square hashing, with an emphasis on designing functions that evenly distribute records.

The research portion examines how students seek help in programming courses, particularly in the context of generative AI. Using datasets from office hours (MyDigitalHand) and online platforms like Piazza, the speaker develops a taxonomy of help-seeking interactions, such as debugging, clarification, and tool usage. A key finding is the disconnect between TA perceptions of helpfulness and student experiences, suggesting a need for more targeted TA training informed by interaction data. The talk also explores how generative AI may shift teaching practices, including more AI-resistant assignments and normalization of help-seeking as part of learning. Overall, the seminar emphasizes positioning students as capable learners and promoting interactive, accessible, and feedback-driven educational environments.

I would recommend Alex Hicks as a faculty member at Virginia Tech. The seminar demonstrates a strong ability to connect foundational computer science concepts with meaningful education research, showing both depth and practical impact. The teaching demo was clear and structured, though at times slightly repetitive in explaining collisions. Hicks’ research is particularly compelling in its use of real student interaction data to inform improvements in teaching practices, especially in adapting to the growing role of generative AI. Hicks would be a valuable addition as both an instructor and research advisor, particularly in computing education and pedagogy.

This seminar explores how computing education can better align with modern software engineering practices, particularly as industry workflows shift toward large-scale codebases and GenAI-assisted development. The speaker critiques the traditional academic model where students complete programming assignments and receive feedback from autograders, since it emphasizes final code artifacts while de-emphasizing the development process such as debugging, testing, and incremental iteration. This disconnect contributes to a growing academia-industry gap, where students are trained to write code from scratch, while real-world engineering focuses on modifying and understanding existing systems with attention to quality, security, and collaboration. The urgency of addressing this gap is illustrated by declining entry-level opportunities and limited internship participation. The speaker highlights several findings on student learning and behavior. Studies on live coding show no measurable improvement in learning outcomes or programming processes, though active variants may increase engagement. A major challenge in junior developers is understanding large codebases, where professionals spend most of their time on comprehension and navigation rather than writing code. Students struggle in this area due to inefficient search habits, poor use of documentation, and difficulty identifying relevant code. Additionally, debugging practices tend to rely heavily on print statements rather than more effective tools like debuggers. The integration of GenAI introduces both opportunities and risks: students often rely on one-shot prompts, reinforcing traditional debugging and testing skills, while trust in AI systems fluctuates—high in the short term but diminishing over time. The speaker suggests that GenAI can also serve as a gateway to teaching core software engineering concepts such as version control and working within large, generated codebases.

This seminar explores how human feedback can be leveraged more effectively in autonomous robot learning using a collaborative learning paradigm. The speaker argues that traditional passive supervision is insufficient for robust generalization and proposes a collaborative learning paradigm in which robots proactively request human assistance when uncertainty is high instead relying on human-initiated corrections. The objective is to iteratively learn a policy that mimics expert behavior, with all interventions incorporated into the training dataset. A key challenge lies in defining a reliable confidence measure so the robot can determine when to ask for help. To address this, the speaker introduces adaptive uncertainty estimation through Online Conformal Prediction, allowing feedback intervals to adjust dynamically based on observed expert input. By combining human-initiated and robot-initiated interventions, the system can better calibrate its uncertainty and improve learning efficiency. Experimental results show that approaches like ConformalDAgger outperform alternatives such as EnsembleDAgger by capturing longer, more informative correction segments and increasing help requests only when necessary. The speaker also highlights the importance of assistive policies for shared autonomy, particularly in mapping low-dimensional human inputs to high-dimensional robot actions. User variability further complicates this process, as different individuals may achieve the same outcome through distinct strategies, necessitating user-calibrated uncertainty estimates. Uncalibrated models struggle with out-of-distribution inputs, reinforcing the need for conformal prediction techniques. Additionally, the speaker emphasizes modeling interaction costs, as requesting help can be more expensive than receiving corrections, and proposes iterative planning to recover from teaching failures.
I would recommend the speaker as a faculty member at Virginia Tech, particularly for research mentorship. Their work demonstrates a strong balance between theoretical rigor and practical application, especially in integrating uncertainty quantification with real-world human-robot interaction. The seminar was well-structured and conveyed complex ideas clearly, particularly in motivating the need for proactive robot learning and adaptive feedback mechanisms. Their research has significant potential impact in robotics and autonomous systems, especially in domains requiring safe and efficient human collaboration. As a research advisor, they would be valuable for students interested in machine learning, robotics, and human-in-the-loop systems, though the depth of the material may require a solid background to fully appreciate.

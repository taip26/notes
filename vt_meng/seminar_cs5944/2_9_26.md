When to use Quantum Computers?

Turing machines = newtonian physics
quantum computers allow us to model certain problems with a large speedup (up to 13000x)

there are quantum algorithms for existing problems that offer a large speedup such as discrete log and factoring, database search, linear systems of equations, etc.

So, why not use quantum for everything?
 - heuristics cannot be tested without large quantum computers
 - need new tools to understand limitations of quantum
 - builds intuition to design new quantum algs


What problems are quantum solvable?
 - is it hard?
 - are there existing algorithms
 - why is it hard

In a typical setting, we must design a chemical construct with quantum chemistry to get the desired end result. Unfortunately, the quantum chemistry part is difficult even with ML
The learning strategy is to parameterize the description of the chemical construct, use quantum computer to simulate the quantum chemistry process, and then train. However, this approach does not work in practice.

In a feed-forward network, we update weights iteratively using training loss, which behaves as a Gaussian process in wide-network limit. To optimize, use second-order methods such as Adam in the loss landscape. In a quantum network, they do not typically perform Gaussian processes.This may be statistically analyzed using physics, but then you would just use classical optimization in this case, so it is quantumly hard. 

There is an area of classically hard, quantumly easy problems, but these only give polynomial advantages, only during inference, and only sometimes.

# Speaker: Eric Anschuetz
This seminar explores when quantum computers should be used and what kinds of problems they can realistically solve. Classical computers can be modeled using Turing machines, analogous to Newtonian physics, while quantum computers leverage quantum mechanics to achieve potentially large speedups for certain problems. Notable examples include algorithms for discrete logarithms, factoring, database search, and solving linear systems of equations. However, quantum computers are not universally applicable. Many heuristics cannot yet be tested without large-scale quantum hardware, and researchers still lack the theoretical tools needed to fully understand the limitations of quantum algorithms. As a result, developing intuition and new analytical frameworks is necessary to determine which problems truly benefit from quantum computation.The seminar also discusses the challenge of identifying quantum-solvable problems. Researchers must determine whether a problem is inherently difficult, whether classical algorithms already exist, and why the problem is hard in the first place. One example comes from quantum chemistry, where designing chemical constructs often requires simulating complex quantum systems. While a potential approach is to parameterize the system and use a quantum computer to simulate the chemistry during training, this strategy is not currently practical. Unlike classical neural networks, whose training behavior can often be approximated by Gaussian processes and optimized using techniques such as second-order methods, quantum networks typically do not exhibit similar statistical properties. In many cases, classical optimization techniques may therefore be more practical. Another theme of the seminar is the search for quantum-classical gaps, where quantum algorithms significantly outperform classical ones. Current evidence suggests that many quantum advantages may only provide polynomial speedups and may only occur during inference rather than training. One promising direction involves studying ground state problems and exploiting the sensitivity of quantum information to reveal classical shadows of quantum states. The seminar concludes by outlining future research directions, including identifying realistic system parameters, constructing quantum memories from hard problem instances, exploring classical hardness beyond known models such as SYK, and designing quantum algorithms that achieve the predicted theoretical advantages.
I would recommend the speaker as a faculty member at Virginia Tech. They present a balanced perspective on quantum computing by clearly outlining both the potential advantages and the current limitations of the field. Rather than focusing solely on theoretical speedups, the seminar emphasizes practical challenges such as the difficulty of training quantum models and identifying meaningful quantum-classical gaps. The speaker demonstrates a strong depth of knowledge in quantum algorithms and computational complexity, while also connecting the topic to applications in chemistry and machine learning. This combination of theoretical understanding and interdisciplinary relevance would make them a valuable research advisor and contributor to the CS department.
